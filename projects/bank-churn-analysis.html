Section 1

Population Overview

Query — Overall churn statistics

-- Overall churn summary
SELECT
    COUNT(*)                                        AS total_customers,
    SUM(churn)                                      AS total_churned,
    COUNT(*) - SUM(churn)                          AS total_retained,
    ROUND(100.0 * SUM(churn) / COUNT(*), 2)         AS churn_rate_pct,
    ROUND(AVG(age),         1)                       AS avg_age,
    ROUND(AVG(credit_score), 1)                       AS avg_credit_score,
    ROUND(AVG(balance),      2)                       AS avg_balance,
    ROUND(AVG(tenure),       1)                       AS avg_tenure
FROM customers;

Base churn rate of 20.4% — materially elevated

One in five customers has left the bank. This is a significant attrition rate for a retail bank where the cost of acquiring a replacement customer typically exceeds the cost of retention by 5–7×. The geographic and demographic breakdowns that follow identify where this risk concentrates.

Germany is a structural outlier

Germany's churn rate of 32.4% is nearly double that of France and Spain. This is not a sampling artifact — Germany represents a meaningful share of the customer base. Whether driven by local competitive dynamics, service gaps, or product fit, Germany demands targeted investigation.

Females churn at a higher rate than males

The gender gap in churn is consistent across geographies (confirmed in the cross-tab). This may reflect differences in product preferences, service experiences, or demographic correlations with age and account type. It warrants disaggregated retention strategy by gender.

Inactive members are the highest behavioural risk signal

Customers who are not actively using their accounts churn at nearly twice the rate of active members. Inactivity is both a leading indicator and a retention intervention point — re-engagement programs targeted at inactive customers before they churn are among the highest-ROI retention tactics.

Critical finding — Age 45-54 is the highest-churn cohort at 49.8%

Mid-career customers (45–54) churn at nearly 2.4× the overall average. This cohort likely holds the highest account balances and longest relationship tenure, making their loss disproportionately costly. They may be at peak financial complexity — re-evaluating their banking relationships as they approach peak earning years. Standard retention messages designed for younger customers may not resonate.

Germany × Female is the most concentrated risk intersection

The country × gender heatmap reveals that German female customers face the highest combined churn rate across all geography-gender combinations. This compounding effect — where both the country-level risk factor and the gender-level risk factor reinforce each other — makes this segment the most critical target for tailored retention intervention.

Tenure does not predict churn meaningfully

Churn rates are broadly flat across all tenure levels (0–10 years), with no clear trend. This is a notable negative finding: long-standing customers are not significantly more loyal than newer ones. Tenure-based loyalty programmes alone are unlikely to materially reduce churn. The drivers appear to be product, financial, and demographic rather than relationship length.

Customers with 3-4 products have catastrophically high churn rates

The product count vs. churn relationship is sharply non-linear and counterintuitive at first glance. Customers with 2 products have the lowest churn rate, while those with 3 or 4 products churn at rates exceeding 80%. This is almost certainly a reverse-causation signal: customers who are already preparing to leave may open additional short-term or transactional products (e.g., a linked savings account to facilitate a transfer) as part of their exit process. Alternatively, customers who are unhappy but feel trapped by multiple products may still eventually leave all at once. Either interpretation has significant implications for cross-sell strategy.

2-product customers are the most stable cohort

Customers with exactly 2 products have the lowest churn rate by a wide margin. The two-product relationship likely represents the natural "sweet spot" — enough diversification to create genuine multi-product engagement without the complexity or dissatisfaction that appears to drive 3+ product churn. This is the target state for retention-focused cross-sell.

Mid-to-high balance customers (100K–200K) are most at risk

Zero-balance customers have a significantly below-average churn rate — their relationship with the bank is likely transactional, with low switching motivation. Churn peaks sharply in the 100K–200K balance band, representing customers with meaningful assets who have options and the financial sophistication to act on dissatisfaction. These customers represent the highest loss of wallet share per departure.

Credit score has weak discriminatory power for churn

Churn rates are broadly similar across all credit score bands. This is a useful negative finding: credit quality alone does not predict who will leave. A bank cannot simply use credit risk scores as a proxy for churn risk — dedicated churn models using behavioural and demographic features are needed. The weak credit score signal also suggests that both high-quality and lower-quality customers are churning at similar rates, which is more consistent with a service/engagement driver than a financial stress driver.

    CASE WHEN churn = 1 THEN 'Churned' ELSE 'Retained' END  AS customer_status,

Profile of a churned customer: older, higher balance, less active

The average churned customer is approximately 4–5 years older than the retained customer, holds a substantially higher account balance (meaning higher financial loss per departure), and is far less likely to be an active member. Crucially, churned customers hold slightly more products on average — reinforcing the hypothesis that multi-product relationships can be a sign of impending exit rather than deep engagement. The average credit scores are nearly identical, confirming credit quality is not a driver.

Section 6

Conclusions & Recommendations

1. Prioritise Germany with a dedicated programme

Germany's 32.4% churn rate — nearly double France and Spain — represents a country-level service or product fit problem, not a customer-segment issue. A root cause investigation into the German market (competitive benchmarking, NPS by segment, product usage patterns) should precede any retention investment.

2. Re-engagement programme for inactive members

Inactive customers churn at nearly 2× the rate of active ones. Targeted re-engagement — personalised product recommendations, fee waivers, proactive outreach — should focus on inactive customers with mid-to-high balances before they reach the decision point. Inactivity is an early-warning signal that can be monitored in near real-time.

3. Redesign cross-sell strategy around the 2-product sweet spot

2-product customers churn the least. Rather than maximising product count through aggressive cross-sell, the strategy should target single-product customers for one complementary product — and investigate why 3+ product customers have such drastically higher churn before deepening those relationships further.

4. Build a dedicated churn model for the 45–54 cohort

This age band has the highest churn rate and likely the highest lifetime value. Generic retention messages will not work. A targeted retention approach — potentially premium banking relationships, wealth management referrals, or life-stage relevant products — is warranted for this high-value, high-risk segment.

5. Credit score is not a churn signal — avoid conflating

The near-flat churn rate across all credit bands is a critical finding for risk teams: do not assume that low-risk (high credit score) customers are also low-churn. Churn prediction requires a separate model and separate data signals from credit risk assessment.

6. Monitor high-balance (100K–200K) customers as VIP risk

This balance band shows the highest churn rate and the highest per-customer financial impact. Dedicated relationship management, proactive service reviews, and personalised retention offers for customers in this tier could deliver outsized ROI relative to mass-market retention spend.
