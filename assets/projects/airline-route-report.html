Executive Summary

This analysis evaluates US domestic airline routes to guide a startup carrier's market entry decision. Using Q1 2019 Bureau of Transportation Statistics data — covering flight operations and ticket purchases across US medium and large airports — routes were assessed on three dimensions: demand volume, financial profitability, and operational reliability.

1. Background & Objective

A startup airline is evaluating which US domestic routes to enter. The organisation operates from medium and large airports, has a brand promise centred on on-time performance, and is willing to trade some profit for schedule reliability.

The analysis addresses four strategic questions:

  1. Demand signal: Which routes carry the highest passenger volume?
  2. Profitability: Which routes generate the largest financial margin?
  3. Operational recommendation: Which routes best balance reliability, occupancy, and return?
  4. Sustainability: How many flights does it take to recover fixed costs on recommended routes?

A logistic regression is also fitted to identify the key variables that drive whether a flight is financially positive, providing a set of KPIs for ongoing performance monitoring.

Datasets

  • Airport Codes — Static reference; filtered to US medium and large airports with valid IATA codes (821 airports)
  • Flight Operations — Per-flight records including origin/destination, delays, distance, occupancy, and cancellation status
  • Ticket Purchases — Individual itinerary records including origin/destination, fares, and passenger counts
  • Scope: Q1 2019 (January 1 – March 31), round-trip routes only
  • Flight records after cleaning: 1,855,005
  • Ticket records after cleaning: 659,630
  • Central dataset (joined): 1,805,121 flight records

2. Data Quality & Treatment

Three data quality issues were identified and corrected before analysis. Each issue, if left untreated, would have distorted cost calculations, revenue estimates, or route rankings.

Issue 1 — Missing delay values

Approximately 55,991 flight records had missing arrival or departure delay values. These rows were removed rather than imputed, as delay is a core variable in both the cost model (late fees) and the route recommendation filter.

Issue 2 — Heavy right skew in delay fields

Both arrival and departure delay exhibited extreme right skew (arrival skew: 9.0, departure skew: 10.1). A small number of extreme delay events — some exceeding 2,900 minutes — would have distorted any mean-based aggregation. Log transformation was applied to all positive delay values, reducing skew to approximately 1.28 (arrival) and 1.38 (departure).

Issue 3 — Passenger count and fare outliers

The passenger field in the tickets data had a skew of 29.9, driven by bulk-ticket records. Log transformation was applied, reducing skew to 2.74. The itinerary fare field was right-skewed (skew: 5.72); values above the 95th percentile were replaced with the median to prevent outlier fares from inflating average route revenue. Post-treatment skew: 0.329.

Key observation The delay scatter plots illustrate the transformation clearly: the raw data fans out to 3,000+ minute extremes, while the log-transformed distribution compresses into a tight linear band — making route-level averages meaningful and comparable.

Key methodology code

4. Route Profitability Model

Profitability was modelled at the individual flight level by summing variable costs and comparing against estimated revenue. Results were then aggregated to the route level for ranking.

Variable cost components

  • Fuel, Oil, Maintenance & Crew (FOMC): $8.00 per mile
  • Depreciation, Insurance & Other (DIO): $1.18 per mile
  • Airport operational costs: $10,000 per flight at large airports; $5,000 at medium airports (applied to both origin and destination)
  • Late fees: $75 per minute of delay beyond a 15-minute threshold (applied to log-transformed delay values)

Revenue components

  • Ticket revenue: Estimated occupancy (occupancy rate × 200 seats) × average round-trip fare per route
  • Baggage revenue: 50% of passengers × $70 per checked bag
# Variable cost per flight flights_ap['fomc'] = flights_ap['distance'] * 8.00 flights_ap['dio'] = flights_ap['distance'] * 1.18 flights_ap['oa_opex'] = np.where(flights_ap['origin_type'] == 'large_airport', 10000, 5000) flights_ap['cost'] = flights_ap[['fomc', 'dio', 'oa_opex', 'da_opex', 'dep_lf', 'arr_lf']].sum(axis=1) # Revenue per flight flights_ap['guest_cnt'] = flights_ap['occupancy_rate'] * 200 flights_ap['baggage_rev'] = flights_ap['guest_cnt'] * 0.5 * 70 flights_ap['itin_revenue'] = flights_ap['guest_cnt'] * flights_ap['avg_trip_fare'] flights_ap['margin'] = flights_ap['revenue'] - flights_ap['cost']

5. Route Recommendations

Profitability alone was not the sole selection criterion. The startup's brand promise centres on punctuality, so routes were additionally filtered for operational reliability. Selection criteria:

  • Average arrival delay < 9 (log-transformed units — corresponding to the 75th percentile of the raw delay distribution)
  • Average departure delay < 9 (same threshold)
  • Average occupancy rate ≥ 0.65 (equal to or above the dataset mean)

The five highest-margin routes satisfying all three criteria form the recommended set.

6. Break-Even Analysis

For each recommended route, the fixed cost recovery threshold was calculated by dividing the total annual fixed cost ($90MM) by the per-flight contribution margin (average revenue minus average variable cost). This represents the minimum number of flights needed to cover all fixed overheads.

# Per-flight averages across all flights on each route brkvn = flights_ap.groupby(['origin', 'destination']).agg( v_cost=('cost', 'mean'), revenue=('revenue', 'mean')) brkvn['break_even'] = (brkvn['f_cost'] / (brkvn['revenue'] - brkvn['v_cost'])).round()

7. Key Performance Drivers — Logistic Regression

To identify which operational factors most influence whether a flight turns a positive return, a binary logistic regression was fitted. The target variable was defined as 1 (profitable) if the flight-level return rate was positive, and 0 (unprofitable) otherwise. Four features were tested:

  • Distance — total route distance in miles
  • Occupancy Rate — proportion of seats filled
  • Departure Delay — log-transformed minutes of departure delay
  • Avg Trip Fare — average round-trip ticket price on the route

8. Methodology & Assumptions

The following assumptions were applied consistently throughout the analysis:

  1. Passenger distribution across operators on the same route is assumed uniform, regardless of flight timing or carrier amenities.
  2. Average ticket pricing is used as a proxy for profitability, without adjusting for cabin class mix.
  3. No seasonality effect is modelled — Q1 2019 results are treated as representative of the full year.
  4. Depreciation is applied as a constant per-mile rate, independent of route length or aircraft age.
  5. "Medium or large airport" is interpreted as any route where both endpoints fall in those airport size categories.
  6. The organisation is willing to accept lower profit margins in exchange for above-average on-time performance.
  7. Fixed cost of $90MM is applied uniformly across all routes for break-even calculation purposes.
Data note Delay fields were log-transformed prior to all downstream calculations, including the late-fee computation and the recommendation filter. Delay thresholds referenced throughout (e.g., "less than 9") apply to the log-transformed scale. All analyses are based on Q1 2019 BTS public data.
